  paraOne: "No single component of a Florida home receives more attention during an inspection than the roof. It is the first line of defense against the state's intense UV exposure, daily summer thunderstorms, and hurricane-force winds. It is also the component most closely scrutinized by insurance carriers when determining whether to write a homeowners policy and at what premium. Understanding what an inspector evaluates on a Florida roof — and what the findings mean for insurance, repair planning, and negotiation — is valuable knowledge for any buyer or homeowner in Southwest Florida.",
  paraTwo: "Florida homes are covered primarily in two roofing materials: concrete or clay tile and asphalt shingles, with flat or low-slope modified bitumen roofing common on additions and some contemporary designs. Asphalt shingles degrade faster in Florida than in northern climates due to extreme heat cycling and UV intensity — a 30-year shingle in Florida may have an effective life of 15 to 20 years. Tile roofs last longer but are subject to cracked tiles, failed mortar at ridges and hips, and failed underlayment beneath the tile that is invisible from the surface but critical to waterproofing. Inspectors evaluate the visible surface condition, age indicators, and any signs of water intrusion in the attic below.",
  title: "What the Inspector Evaluates on a Florida Roof",
  paraThree: "A thorough roof inspection includes walking the roof where it is safely accessible, evaluating the covering material for condition and age indicators, inspecting all penetrations including plumbing vents, HVAC equipment, and skylights, examining the flashing at walls and valleys, checking gutters and downspouts for proper drainage, and inspecting the attic for signs of past or active leaking, moisture staining, and adequate ventilation. The inspector also notes the roof shape — whether it is hip, gable, flat, or a combination — because roof shape is a primary factor in the Wind Mitigation Inspection form and directly affects hurricane insurance premiums.",

Citizens Property Insurance Corporation follows specific roof age guidelines that affect policy eligibility. For buyers, this means that a home with an aging roof may come with a significantly limited insurance market and higher premiums even if the roof is not visibly failing.
